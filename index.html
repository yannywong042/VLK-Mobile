Case Study

Client Mobile AI Assistant

Van Lanschot Kempen is a premier private bank where clients demand the highest levels of personalization and trust. In this context, I was responsible for the 0-to-1 experience design of the Mobile AI Assistant, ensuring that this high-end trust remained intact while integrating AI.


I architected a scalable design system comprising 160+ specialized AI components and defined an inline contextual AI workflow that allows seamless interaction without breaking the user’s financial overview. To ensure technical alignment and high-fidelity execution, I delivered a comprehensive 17-page standardized design specification (Format Documentation). This systematic approach successfully minimized user friction and mitigated AI hallucinations by integrating human-in-the-loop feedback mechanisms.

Outcome

Context proximity determines guidance density

Full-page and modal layouts require explicit scope-setting, example prompts, and step-by-step onboarding. In contrast, inline panels embedded within a task view can rely on the surrounding UI to carry that context, allowing the Start Page to be significantly simplified.

Inline and floating formats are the preferred entry points for task-embedded AI

Stakeholders reached consensus that for context-aware AI, embedded side panels and floating modals outperform full-screen takeovers.

Prompt suggestions must be adaptive

Participants wanted the Start Page to surface dynamically generated suggestions rather than open-ended starters like "ask me anything." Equally important, the density and complexity of suggestions should adapt to the user's AI proficiency and job function.

Design

Design Principle

I applied Atomic Design to accelerate the design process and ensure consistency with our existing main design system. The system strikes a balance between flexibility (supporting diverse AI interaction patterns) and restraint (clear rules about when not to use AI components), making it both developer-friendly and user-considerate.

Don't Just Remove the Tool — Replace It see more

Our product owner raised a security concern about Token Studio, a third-party Figma plugin we'd been using. Since we're in private banking, security is non-negotiable, so the plugin had to go.

The problem was that the original plan was our engineers will rely on it heavily. Removing it would slow them down, and I knew that wouldn't go over well.

I dug into how Token Studio actually worked and discovered that its data could be transferred into Figma's native variables and exported as a standard JSON file. That gave engineers the same structured output they needed, without any third-party dependency.

Iteration: Prompt fluency and Output literacy

Based on user testing results, two core optimization directions have been identified: prompt fluency and output literacy, aimed at lowering the barrier to entry for users while enhancing the transprancy d controllability of AI responses.

Takeaways

When AI can do everything for users, what is UX?

In the context of AI products specifically, UX takes on new stakes: the AI can act, not just respond. That shifts the designer's job from organizing information to governing behavior. And it's important to aware of the boundaries that we should not lose the foundation of design ability, instead making AI more like a workflow, not outsourcing everything to it.

What I did well: Behavioral contract

At scale, the behavioral contract becomes a shared artifact, a single source of truth that product, security and engineering can argue about early. I clarified what AI may do, what it must never do, what it must ask before doing, and how it should explain itself.

What I can improve: Review AI actions

One gap is in how clearly the boundaries of AI behavior are defined at the workflow level. In a complex system, it's not enough to state what the AI may or may not do, the harder design problem is mapping when those rules apply across a multi-step flow.
